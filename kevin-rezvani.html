Interview with Kevin rezvani

Interviewer
Kevin, your work often explores themes of memory and nostalgia through digital media. What draws you to these temporal concepts?
Kevin Rezvani
Memory is such a strange thing—it's both incredibly personal and completely unreliable. I'm fascinated by how we construct our pasts, how we edit and curate our memories, and how digital technology is changing the way we remember and forget.
Interviewer
Your recent project used AI to generate "false memories" based on user input. What was the thinking behind this approach?
Kevin Rezvani
I wanted to explore how easily our memories can be manipulated, how vulnerable we are to suggestion and reconstruction. The AI wasn't creating lies—it was creating possibilities, alternative versions of events that could have happened. It's about the fluidity of truth and the power of narrative.
Interviewer
How do you see technology changing our relationship with the past?
Kevin Rezvani
We're living in an age of infinite documentation, where every moment can be captured and preserved. But that doesn't mean we understand it any better. Sometimes I think we're drowning in data but starving for meaning. My work is about finding poetry in that paradox.
