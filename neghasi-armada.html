Interview with Neghasi armada

Interviewer
Neghasi, your work often incorporates elements of Afrofuturism and speculative fiction. What draws you to these themes?
Neghasi Armada
Afrofuturism isn't just about imagining the future—it's about reclaiming the past and reimagining the present. I'm interested in creating worlds where Black people aren't just surviving, but thriving, where we're not just present in the future, but shaping it.
Interviewer
Your recent series explores themes of diaspora and belonging. How do you approach these complex emotional landscapes?
Neghasi Armada
The diaspora experience is about being from everywhere and nowhere at the same time. There's this constant negotiation of identity, of trying to hold onto traditions while adapting to new contexts. My work is about finding beauty and power in that complexity, in that in-between space.
Interviewer
What role do you see art playing in social change and activism?
Neghasi Armada
Art can't change the world by itself, but it can change how people see the world. It can create empathy, spark conversations, and help people imagine possibilities they hadn't considered. Sometimes the most radical thing you can do is to show people a different way of being human.
